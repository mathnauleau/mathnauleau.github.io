Shifting design from bottleneck to strategic leverage

Date
2024 – 2026

Client / Brands
Eneco & Oxxio

Result
Spark Design System (matured)

Tools

When five squads rebuilt the app simultaneously, design became the bottleneck. I took a DesignOps approach—shifting focus from execution to operations—and created the conditions where designers could do their best work and shape team culture.

Overview

I joined Eneco at a critical moment: five product squads rebuilding the app in parallel, and design was already the slowdown. My mission was to mature the Spark Design System—but also to diagnose and fix why design had become a bottleneck in the first place. The insight was simple: the issue wasn't the designers. It was the operating model. Process shapes culture, and culture shapes output. By combining design system work with a DesignOps mindset, I created the space for designers to think strategically and freed myself to focus on leverage over execution.

Symptoms & Frictions

#1 Designers with no time to think

Designers created ideas in 1–2 days while developers waited. There was no time for research, problem framing, ideation, or prototyping. Once design was finished, it went straight to implementation without any design review. The bottleneck was real.

#2 Designing straight into high-fidelity

We believed speed meant skipping exploration. Designers jumped to high-fidelity mockups without evidence, without challenging assumptions. The designer role became a graphics operator, not a problem solver.

#3 Little shared expertise or peer review

Designers felt isolated in squads with no time for knowledge sharing. Peer review for consistency and standards was a luxury we couldn't afford. Guilds and communities of practice never took root.

#4 No coordination across squads

Without design velocity, time was allocated day-to-day. Designers couldn't say no to product managers. Squads competed rather than collaborated. PMs ring-fenced their designer as a resource, not a partner.

#5 Low-quality delivery

Designers never reviewed code. When designs shipped, they rarely matched the original. We were trying to define the design system while building the app simultaneously—a critical mistake.

#6 No trust in the design system

In six months, we'd shipped two components. Many compromises, confusing setup, and designers weren't even happy with the results. The design system was a liability, not a foundation.

The Approach

My insight was that design wasn't broken—the operating model was broken. I combined design system work with DesignOps thinking: process shapes mindset, mindset shapes output. By creating space for designers to think, building peer review into routine, and automating manual overhead, I shifted my own role from execution to operations. The goal: design that scales leverage, not headcount.

What We Did

Three buckets of work: Design Practice, Design System, and Automation.

Design Practice

  • Moved designers one sprint ahead so they had time to research, ideate, and critique
  • Documented our design process and handed ownership back to the team
  • Hosted bi-weekly design critiques to make peer review routine, not optional
  • Ran workshops on components, accessibility, and proper design handoffs
  • Onboarded three new designers with clear frameworks and cultural foundations

Design System

  • Consolidated four icon libraries into one coherent system
  • Established a clear, repeatable process for adding new components
  • Set documentation standards and created a contribution framework
  • Introduced Frontify as the bridge between brand and product design
  • Restructured the Figma workspace to support collaboration across teams
  • Started a token repository to share foundations across iOS, web, and platforms

Automation & Tooling

  • Built Claude skills to automate component documentation—eliminated weeks of manual work
  • Created GitHub Actions workflows for design system contribution workflows
  • Built Figma utilities and plugins to speed up design work
  • Set up a GitHub UX repository to test vibe-coding and experimental ideas
  • Launched monthly newsletters and demos to keep visibility high

Outcomes

Three shifts happened—one visible, one cultural, one strategic.

  • Design system became trusted. From "liability" to "resource." Designers used it, believed in it, contributed to it. Documentation was clear. Decisions were defensible.
  • Team culture improved. Designers regained agency. They could say no. They could push back. They could spend time thinking instead of just executing. Peer review became routine.
  • Leadership scaled. By automating and systematizing, I shifted from "doing design" to "designing how design happens." My role moved from execution to operations, freeing capacity for strategy.

I've learned that

  • DesignOps is fundamentally about culture. Process shapes mindset, mindset shapes output.
  • A good design system defines the quality of everything downstream—including AI output, handoffs, and team decision-making.
  • Value doesn't lie in individual execution. It lies in connecting ideas, people, and systems to amplify what everyone can do.
  • Operations isn't just about fixing problems. It's a practice for shaping how your team thinks and works together.

What's next

The vision is clear: a design system that's not just used by teams—but actively consumed by AI agents, development tools, and automated pipelines. A system that scales leverage, not headcount. Every component, token, and pattern flows through code generators, accessibility checkers, and design-to-code pipelines. Consistency isn't policed by humans. It's baked into automation. That's the real maturity: a system smart enough to amplify human creativity instead of replace it.
